## Site Restructure Plan

### The Problem with the Current Site

The current fireflysoftware.dev is optimized for Montana locals and referral traffic. A coastal visitor arriving cold encounters several signals that undercut the studio positioning:

- The nav lists five service items including "Site Rescue" and "Outfitters" — reads as a local repair shop and regional vertical, not a capable studio
- "Site Rescue" and "Website Maintenance" as top-level nav items position Firefly as a fixer rather than a builder
- The "what brings you here" section reads like a help desk menu — useful for locals who already trust Firefly, but not credentialing for someone who just found the site
- The About page leads with "One person. Start to finish." — no longer accurate with Jaden on the team, and the framing skews toward reassurance rather than confidence
- The footer lists "Service Areas: Helena, MT / All of Montana" — actively signals Firefly doesn't work outside Montana
- The stats block (5+ years, 3 services, 24/7 developer on call) doesn't carry credential weight for a cold visitor

### Revised Navigation

**Current:** Services (Website Development, Website Maintenance, Site Rescue, Custom Software, Outfitters) · About · Pricing · Portfolio · Posts · Contact Us

**Revised:** Work · Services · Process · About · Contact

**Rationale:** Fewer top-level items signals focus. "Portfolio" becomes "Work" — a more confident framing. "Pricing" moves off the nav and onto individual service pages as a section. "Posts" stays accessible but isn't primary nav real estate. "Process" is added as a top-level page — it's the primary trust-builder for a cold coastal visitor evaluating a remote studio.

### Revised Page Map

```
/ (Home)
/work
/services
  /websites
  /software
/process
/about
/contact
```

Six pages. Site Rescue folds into the Websites service page as a mentioned capability. Website Maintenance becomes the "Ongoing Support" lane on the home page and a section on the Websites service page. Outfitters moves out of the main nav — that vertical is handled by Packstring.

### Page-by-Page Rationale

**Home** — Restructured to lead with credentialing before self-sorting. Cold coastal visitors need the 30-second case for Firefly before they pick a service lane. The four-quadrant "what brings you here" section is compressed to three lanes and reframed from help-desk menu to studio capability statement.

**Work** — Renamed from "Portfolio." Each project entry leads with the client's problem, not the deliverables. Format: Problem → Approach → Result. Metrics included wherever available. This is how coastal clients read case studies.

**Services / Websites** — Opens with the client's frustration with templates and platform lock-in. Folds in Site Rescue and Maintenance as capabilities rather than separate products. Includes a pricing anchor ("starting at").

**Services / Software** — Opens with the off-the-shelf problem. Addresses scope and reliability concerns directly — the anxieties a coastal client has about hiring a remote solo-ish studio for a software project. Includes pricing approach.

**Process** — New page. The primary trust engine for coastal clients. Named steps with client responsibilities at each stage, communication expectations, and a plain answer to "what happens if something goes wrong?" This page replaces the sales call for clients who don't want a sales call.

**About** — Retains Logan's backstory (Air Force, ATC, missionary kid — all of it). Adds Jaden as a team member. Reframes from "One person. Start to finish." to "Small team. Full accountability." The "why small businesses" section expands slightly to speak to clients who aren't in Montana.

**Contact** — Short paragraph above the form setting expectations: response time, what happens next, and a line that makes it safe to reach out without a fully formed project.

### Footer Change

Remove "Service Areas: Helena, MT / All of Montana."

Replace with: *"Based in Helena, Montana. Working with clients across the US."*

One sentence. Removes the geographic ceiling without making a production of it.

### The "We" Shift

With Jaden on the team, the site moves from "I" to "we" — but carefully. Logan's personal backstory on the About page stays in first person. Everywhere else, "we" is used, but the small-team reality is named directly rather than obscured. The goal is: small by design, not small by accident.

## Page Copy — Process

### Header

**How we work**

Every project is different. The process isn't. Here's exactly what working with Firefly looks like — from the first call to the day your site goes live.

---

### Framing

One thing you'll notice throughout: we do the work first, then ask for your reaction. You're the expert on your business. We're the experts on the build. Our job is to bring you something finished enough to have an opinion about — not to pepper you with questions and put the blank page in your lap.

---

### Discover

The first conversation is a scoping call — no sales pitch, no deck. We talk through what you need, what you've tried, and what success looks like for your business. If we're a good fit, we'll tell you. If we're not, we'll tell you that too.

By the end of this call you'll have a clear sense of scope, timeline, and what it's likely to cost.

---

### Agree

If the scoping call goes well, we put it in writing. We send over a project proposal that covers scope, deliverables, timeline, and price — in plain English, not legal boilerplate. No work starts until both sides have signed.

This protects you as much as it protects us. You know exactly what you're getting before any money changes hands.

---

### Research

Before we open a design tool, we research your market, identify what successful competitors are doing well, and establish what good looks like in your space. Then we build two or three brand and UI direction examples — different enough to be a real choice, grounded enough to be buildable.

You pick a direction and tell us what you like and what you don't. That's the whole ask. No mood boards to assemble, no questionnaires to fill out. We bring the options; you steer.

---

### Build

Once a direction is agreed, we build. You get a public URL from day one — you can watch the site take shape without scheduling a call or waiting for a demo. We work through the core pages first: landing page, services, about, contact.

When the first full draft is ready, we send you a list of every factual claim the site makes about your business — guarantees, credentials, years of experience, service areas, all of it. You go down the list and tell us what to change. Most owners don't have time to write copy from scratch, and it's faster for everyone if you're correcting something finished than building it from nothing.

---

### Refine

This is where the site gets finished. You've seen the full draft, you've verified the claims, and now you have something real to react to. Most clients find it easier to say what they want to change once they can see what they have.

We go through as many rounds of edits as the project needs — copy, layout, visuals, mobile behavior. When you're satisfied, we move to launch.

---

### Launch

Before we go live we run a full pre-launch checklist — performance, security, mobile, SEO basics, contact forms, analytics. Then we flip the switch.

After launch you receive a written summary of everything that was built and delivered: pages, features, integrations, credentials, and anything you'll need to know going forward. You own all of it — the code, the domain, the content. We don't hold anything hostage.

---

### What happens if something goes wrong?

Scope changes. Timelines slip. Clients change their minds — it happens on every project and it's not a crisis. If scope grows beyond what the proposal covers, we'll tell you before we build it, not after. If a deadline is at risk, you'll hear from us first. We'd rather have an uncomfortable conversation early than a bad surprise late.

We've never abandoned a project. We don't intend to start.

## Visual Design Direction

### The Concept

**"Precision instrument."**

Firefly isn't a creative agency and it isn't a freelancer. It's a small, technically excellent studio that builds things that work. The visual language should feel like the product of someone who cares deeply about craft but has no interest in showing off. Think less "agency portfolio" and more "well-made tool" — the kind of design where everything is exactly where it needs to be and nothing is there for decoration.

The one decorative exception: the firefly itself. Bioluminescence in a dark field is the single moment of Wilderness Wonder the visual system gets. Use it sparingly. When it appears it should feel earned.

---

### Color System

**Dark (above the fold)**

| Role | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Background | `#0E0F0D` | Near-black with a faint warm undertone — not pure black, not blue-black. Think late Montana night sky. |
| Surface | `#161810` | Cards, nav, elevated elements |
| Border | `#2A2D26` | Subtle dividers |
| Text primary | `#F0EDE6` | Warm off-white — not clinical |
| Text secondary | `#8A8C82` | Muted, readable |
| Accent | `#C8F060` | Firefly glow — yellow-green bioluminescence. Used once per section maximum. CTAs, hover states, the logo mark. |

**Light (body sections)**

| Role | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Background | `#F7F5F0` | Warm natural white — parchment, not sterile |
| Surface | `#FFFFFF` | Cards and elevated elements |
| Border | `#E2DDD6` | Warm grey |
| Text primary | `#1A1C18` | Near-black matching dark bg undertone |
| Text secondary | `#6B6D63` | Muted body text |
| Accent | `#C8F060` | Same glow accent — consistent across both modes |

The accent color is the thread that runs through both halves of the page. It appears on the dark hero as a glow, and in the light body as a sharp, confident pop. It never appears as a background fill — only as text, borders, underlines, and hover states.

---

### Typography

**Display / Headlines:** `Bebas Neue` — all caps, condensed, high authority. Used for page headers and section labels only. Pairs the Montana utility register perfectly — terse, declarative, takes up exactly the space it needs.

**Body / UI:** `Instrument Sans` — clean, slightly warm, excellent at small sizes. Not Inter. Has just enough personality to feel considered without drawing attention to itself.

**Mono / Code / Coordinates:** `IBM Plex Mono` — already in the Firefly system. Used for the coordinates (`46.5958°N · 112.0270°W`), inline technical references, and any code snippets. Signals technical depth without effort.

**Scale (mobile-first, fluid):**

| Token | Size | Usage |
|---|---|---|
| `--text-display` | 4.5rem–7rem fluid | Page hero headline |
| `--text-h1` | 2.5rem–3.5rem fluid | Section headers |
| `--text-h2` | 1.5rem–2rem fluid | Sub-section headers |
| `--text-body` | 1rem–1.125rem | Body copy |
| `--text-small` | 0.8125rem | Labels, metadata, coordinates |

Line height for body: `1.7`. Line height for display: `0.95` — tight, intentional, confident.

---

### Spacing & Layout

**Max content width:** `1180px` — not full-bleed, not narrow. Comfortable reading width with room to breathe on wide screens.

**Section padding:** `120px` vertical on desktop, `72px` on mobile. Generous. Premium layouts don't cram.

**Grid:** 12-column CSS grid for the body. The dark hero section uses a single centered column — stark, focused.

**One layout rule:** The hero headline is the largest thing on the page. Nothing else competes with it. Every other element earns its size relative to that anchor.

---

### The Hero Section

Dark background. The Sleeping Giant SVG or a minimal topographic line motif sits at low opacity in the background — texture, not illustration. The headline in `Bebas Neue` at display size, warm white. Subhead in `Instrument Sans`, secondary text color. Two CTAs below — primary CTA uses the accent glow color as a border (not filled), secondary is plain text with an arrow.

The coordinates (`46.5958°N · 112.0270°W`) appear in `IBM Plex Mono` at small size, muted — grounding the section in place without making a production of it.

No hero image. No stock photography. No background video. The typography does the work.

---

### Navigation

Dark background matching the hero. Logo left, nav items right. Clean, no mega-menus. Active state uses the accent color as an underline only — 2px, bottom of the text, not a background highlight.

On mobile: full-screen overlay nav, dark background, large `Bebas Neue` nav items. The firefly glow accent on the active item. Simple open/close.

---

### Transition — Dark to Light

The break between the dark hero and the light body sections is not a hard cut. A subtle gradient fade — `#0E0F0D` to `#F7F5F0` over about `80px` — softens the transition. Below that line, the page stays warm white through all content sections.

---

### Components

**Cards (Work, Services):** White surface, `1px` warm border, `4px` border-radius. No drop shadows — shadows make things look like web templates. Hover state: accent color border replaces the warm grey. Subtle `translateY(-2px)` on hover.

**CTAs / Buttons:** Two styles only.
- Primary: accent color border, accent color text, transparent fill. On hover: accent fill, dark text.
- Secondary: no border, text with `→` arrow, accent color on hover. No other button styles.

**Section labels:** Small `Bebas Neue` all-caps label above section headers — e.g., `WHAT WE BUILD` or `HOW IT WORKS`. Accent color. Tracks slightly wider than normal (`letter-spacing: 0.15em`). This is the one place Bebas Neue appears at small size — it works because it's a label, not a headline.

**Testimonial cards:** Warm white background, left border `3px` accent color. Quote in `Instrument Sans` italic, attribution in small caps. No giant quotation mark graphics.

**Dividers:** Not horizontal rules. Section breaks are handled by spacing alone, or by a single-pixel accent color line used once per page maximum.

---

### Motion

Restrained. The site should feel fast and deliberate, not animated.

- **Hero headline:** Single fade-up on load, `0.6s ease-out`. Nothing else animates on load.
- **Scroll reveals:** Sections fade in as they enter the viewport — `opacity 0 → 1`, `translateY 16px → 0`, `0.4s ease-out`. Staggered for card grids.
- **Hover states:** `0.15s` transition on all interactive elements. Fast enough to feel responsive, not so fast it feels twitchy.
- **No parallax.** No looping animations. No cursor effects.

---

### What to Avoid

- Drop shadows on cards or buttons
- Gradient fills on buttons or backgrounds (the hero-to-body transition gradient is the one exception)
- Rounded corners above `6px`
- Stock photography anywhere
- Purple, blue, or teal accent colors — every dev agency uses these
- Full-bleed background images
- Animated counters on stats
- Anything that requires a loading screen

---

### The One Memorable Thing

Every good site has one thing people remember. For Firefly it should be the moment the accent color appears — that first `#C8F060` glow against the near-black hero, next to the coordinates in mono type. It should feel like watching a firefly light up in a dark field. Quiet, specific, earned.

Everything else on the site exists to not compete with that moment.
